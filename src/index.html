About me

As a seasoned computational biologist with a Ph.D. in Quantitative and Systems Biology from the University of California, Merced, I specialize in multi-omics analysis, leveraging statistical and machine-learning models to solve complex biological problems. Classically trained in biology, I also have extensive experience conducting biological experiments, ranging from eukaryotic single-cell organisms to immune cellular data from mice. My hands-on experience includes using DB Facs Aria LSR and cell sorting machines, which has honed my technical skills in cellular analysis. My scientific journey has equipped me with a profound understanding of the interplay between genomics, proteomics, and transcriptomics, particularly in the context of disease mechanisms. Professionally, I have worked at notable institutions such as Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory and the Joint Genome Institute, where I led projects that harnessed cloud and high-performance computing resources to identify chimeric sequences, predict antibody binding, and forecast viral variants and functions. At PhageNet and in collaboration with Emory University, I have worked extensively with bacterial genomes and human transcriptomic, proteomic, and metabolic data to study type 2 diabetes. Currently, I am exploring the use of generative pre-trained models to identify novel cell types and infer perturbed gene regulatory networks in type 2 diabetes. My interdisciplinary experience spans from foundational biological research to advanced computational modeling, enabling me to bridge the gap between experimental and computational biology effectively. I am driven by the potential to translate complex biological data into actionable insights, ultimately aiming to advance our understanding of disease mechanisms and contribute to the development of innovative therapeutic strategies.

The Dynamics of IL-2 Deficiency: A Mathematical Exploration of Autoimmune Pathology

Autoimmune diseases, driven by an immune system's inability to distinguish self- from foreign-antigens, present a complex interplay of factors whose pathogenesis is not fully understood. In response to this challenge, we developed a mathematical model to emulate the progression of systemic autoimmune disease that was fit to experimental data from spontaneous autoimmune reactions in the IL-2 deficient BALB/c mouse model (IL-2 knockout, KO). Our model simulates the dynamic interactions of IL-2 cytokine with CD4 T cells originating from both the thymus and spleen, which are crucial for maintaining immune tolerance and preventing autoimmune disease dysregulation. Our model-based comparative analysis identified deficient expansion trajectories in naive and regulatory T cells within the autoimmune state compared to the wild-type counterpart. A sensitivity analysis, using Latin Hypercube Sampling, elucidates early Treg dysfunction, critical parameters necessary for preventing autoimmune disease and dissects the systemic implications of IL-2 deficiency. This is the first published mathematical model that explores early autoimmune pathogenesis in IL-2 knockout mice, introducing a new way to study autoimmune development.

Multi-Omics Integration with xMWAS Reveals Immune Dynamics in Type 2 Diabetes

Despite the significant health impacts of Type 2 diabetes (T2D), the underlying mechanisms affecting pancreatic islet beta cells remain poorly understood. This study applied a multi-omics approach to analyze the transcriptional and biochemical profiles of islet cells from human donors with documented T2D compared to non-diabetic controls. The individual analyses identified 25 differentially abundant transcripts, 30 metabolites, and 30 proteins between the groups after FDR adjustment. Metabolomics pathway analysis indicated reduced amino acid and lipid metabolism in T2D islets. Integrated network analysis revealed disruptions in biochemical interactions in T2D islets, with reduced overall connectivity but the emergence of tightly correlated feature clusters. Key differences included altered immune activity, fatty acid metabolism, and the presence of industrial additives and an herbicide, which were more influential in the T2D network. Differences were also observed in features related to amino acid metabolism and mitochondrial function. This study highlights the potential of multi-omics profiling to identify candidate molecules and mechanisms distinguishing islet cell activity in T2D, which may serve as therapeutic targets to improve beta cell function and mitigate T2D progression.

Charactization of novel cell types by generative pretrained model: scGPT

Identifying unique cellular populations using RNA-Seq data is time-consuming and expertise-dependent. To address this, I am leveraging generative pre-trained models like single-cell GPT to characterize cellular heterogeneity from single-cell RNA-Seq data. This approach streamlines the identification of unique cellular populations using data science methods. I am currently writing an F32 grant proposal to use these models for identifying heterogeneity in beta cells associated with Type 2 diabetes. Due to the constant stress beta cells endure, their cellular states fluctuate, making them difficult to characterize. Generative models like single-cell GPT can effectively identify different cellular populations and mitigate batch effects, offering deeper insights into beta cell heterogeneity and contributing to better therapeutic strategies.

Charactization of novel cell types by generative pretrained model: scGPT

To implement single-cell GPT for characterizing heterogeneity in beta cells of individuals with Type 2 diabetes, I am downloading and analyzing large-scale single-cell RNA-Seq data. This process involves demonstrating that batch effects significantly impact cellular population types. Initial datasets are sourced from PANC-DB. Currently, I have analyzed data from only three individuals, yielding 300 identified beta cells. For a large-scale study, more cellular data is needed. Therefore, I am enhancing the pipeline to handle large datasets efficiently, considering computational limitations and ensuring robust statistical conclusions.
